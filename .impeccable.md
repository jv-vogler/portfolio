## Design Context

### Users

**Primary: hiring eyes** — recruiters, engineering leads, technical hiring managers landing on the site with 60-90 seconds of attention. They scan to evaluate: taste, technical depth, communication, attention to detail. The site itself is the proof — "if you let JV design something, this is what you get."

**Secondary: fellow developers reading the blog** — peers unstuck on a tutorial or hunting for João's voice on a specific topic. The blog is supporting evidence for the primary audience, but a destination of its own for this group.

**Context of use**: low-light dev environments, mid-day desktop sessions between meetings, the occasional phone read of a blog post. Not consumer browsing.

**Job to be done (hiring eyes)**: form a fast, confident judgment about JV's seniority and craft. They are not reading carefully — they are reacting to first impressions and then validating with one or two project pages.

**Job to be done (blog readers)**: get unstuck on a concept, or read a piece of opinionated technical writing in João's voice without being insulted by AI-generated filler.

### Brand Personality

**Three words: precise · playful · opinionated.**

The voice is already documented at length in `.claude/skills/portfolio-voice/voice-profile.md` — that file is canonical for prose. The design layer must leave room for that voice to land: marginalia, footnotes, dry asides, structural callbacks. A glassy SaaS aesthetic would suffocate it; a too-loud editorial brutalism would compete with it.

**Emotional goals for visitors:**

- **Hiring eyes:** quiet confidence. The reaction we want is "this person is unhurried and rigorous" — never "this person is shouting at me."
- **Blog readers:** the calm of a well-set page. The reading experience itself is part of the argument.

### Aesthetic Direction

**North star: Engineer's lab notebook.**

Archival serif display paired with a functional monospace, asymmetric grid, hand-numbered sections, marginalia and footnotes treated as real structure (not decoration), references that look like real citations. The site reads like a careful scientist's notebook that happens to be online.

**Theme: dark-only.** Hard-coded `<html className="dark">` stays. Audience and content (long-form technical reading in dev contexts) make dark the right answer; pretending light mode is a future requirement would only soften decisions.

**Color direction:**

- **Background**: deep ink-black, slightly tinted toward the brand hue (currently a green-leaning tint at `oklch(0.14 0.01 180)` in the hero — keep tinted, but the existing teal-tilt may want re-anchoring around the actual accent hue, not against it).
- **Foreground**: paper-cream (warm off-white), never pure `oklch(0.985 0 0)`. The current `--foreground` reads as flat-light against ink — needs a touch of warmth so long-form prose stops feeling like a CRT.
- **Accent**: the existing vivid green at `oklch(0.65 0.24 155)` is workable as an accent but currently carries too much of the page. In the lab-notebook direction, accent ink appears at one moment per screen — section numbers, the hero name, a single emphasized link. Not on every CTA, every hover, every focus ring.

**Typography direction:**

- Current pair (Poppins + Fira Code) is competent but unspecific. Poppins is on the impeccable reflex-reject list — it makes any design read like a dev-portfolio template. The lab-notebook direction wants a distinctive **archival serif display** (something with real ink — Tiempos, Suisse Works, or a more obscure pick from Pangram Pangram / Klim / ABC Dinamo) and a **functional, slightly characterful mono** (Berkeley Mono, MD IO, JetBrains Mono Pro — not Fira Code).
- Proposed pair shape: serif display for headings + page numbers, mono for code and metadata, and a clean humanist sans (NOT Inter, NOT Plus Jakarta, NOT DM Sans — look further) ONLY for body if the chosen serif doesn't work at body size.
- Modular scale, ~1.25 ratio, fluid `clamp()` on display sizes. Body capped at 65-72ch.

**Reference vibes (mood, not direct copy):**

- Bret Victor's site — long-form, footnoted, asymmetric, takes itself seriously.
- Tufte CSS — sidenotes treated as real structure.
- Frank Chimero's writing pages — restraint with specific moments of warmth.
- A printed lab notebook or museum exhibition catalog — hand-numbered sections, proper citations, marginalia.

**Anti-references (what this must NOT look like):**

- Generic dev-portfolio templates (`<hero with photo + 4 skill icons + project grid + footer>`).
- Vercel-with-gradients / AI-startup glassmorphism / cyan-on-dark "futuristic" palettes.
- Medium and every blog template that descends from it.
- Centered-everything landing pages with rounded-rectangle "feature cards" in 3-column grids.
- The terminal-skin trap: monospace everywhere, blinking cursors on every label, ASCII art used decoratively. The current site flirts with this; the lab-notebook direction is _adjacent_ to it, not the same thing. Mono is a tool, not a costume.

**Accessibility commitments:**

- WCAG 2.2 AA on contrast, focus, motion. The codebase already respects `prefers-reduced-motion` in the hero — extend this discipline everywhere new motion is added.
- Reading width and font size must remain comfortable for users who zoom to 200%.
- All interactive surfaces keyboard-reachable; focus rings visible and intentional, not just the default ring color.
- Color is never the only signal for state.

### Design Principles

These five principles take precedence over visual preferences. Every design decision must defend itself against them.

1. **Long-form is the centerpiece.** Body type, line length, leading, and rhythm get designed first. The hero serves the blog and project pages; not the other way around. If a flourish on the home page makes long-form harder to read, the flourish loses.

2. **Marginalia is structure, not decoration.** Section numbers, footnotes, and side annotations are navigational and rhetorical — they tell the reader what kind of document they're in. They must be implemented as real semantic content, not absolute-positioned ornaments. If they could be removed without the reader noticing, they don't belong.

3. **Type does the heavy lifting.** Hierarchy comes from typographic contrast (size, weight, family, italic), not from chrome (boxes, shadows, borders, tints). When a component feels like it needs a card to stand out, the type is failing — fix the type first.

4. **Asymmetric over centered.** Default to left-aligned, intentionally unbalanced compositions. Centering is reserved for moments where it earns the symmetry — never as a fallback. Break the grid only with purpose.

5. **Quiet by default, loud once.** Each screen earns one moment of intensity (a display heading, an accent ink callout, a single illustration). The rest is restraint. The accent green at full saturation appears at most once per viewport. Decoration without purpose is removed in the polish pass — see `/impeccable polish` and `/impeccable distill`.
